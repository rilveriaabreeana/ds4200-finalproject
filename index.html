Project Overview

Socioeconomic status influences access to opportunities, support systems, and educational resources. This website presents a set of visualizations that examine how those factors relate to student performance.

Socioeconomic status (SES) is a measure of an individual's or family's economic and social position, determined by income, education level, and occupation. SES has been shown to have wide-reaching effects on access to resources, physical and mental health, housing, life outcomes, and education. With education specifically, children of a lower SES often have significant achievement gaps with their peers. This stems from resource inequality, attending schools with less resources, financial and other outside stressors, and less support. It is therefore a pressing issue to address these challenges and close the socioeconomic gap in the classroom. By better understanding the relationships between socioeconomic factors and academic performance, we can identify the best intervention points that would have the most impact. Policymakers would be better informed when making decisions or creating new programs. By exploring a dataset of student attributes and their academic outcomes, this project will help to improve education equity and help at-risk students.

About the Data

Dataset source, size and attributes used in this analysis

Dataset Summary

This project uses a student performance data set obtained from Kaggle to explore the factors that may influence academic outcomes of students. The dataset captures a wide range of socioeconomic, environmental, and educational factors that are commonly associated with student success and has a particular focus on how these factors have an impact on student's test scores. Some of the different variables covered in this dataset include, parental involvement, access to learning resources, internet access, number of tutoring sessions, school type, parental education level as well as student's test scores.

Why This Data Fits the Project

There are 6608 rows in this data set and 20 attributes, providing a sufficiently large and multifaceted sample of examining patterns in academic performance across different student demographics. By focusing specifically on socioeconomic indicators, this project aims to explore which factors are most strongly associated with differences in student performance and whether certain combinations of resources or support structures correspond with higher or lower test scores. The goal is to better understand how disparities in access and opportunity may translate into measurable differences in academic outcomes.

Visualizations

Visualization 1: Exam Score by Multiple Socioeconomic Factors

Altair

Takeaway

Across the four socioeconomic factors of Access to Resources, Family Income, Parental Involvement, and Teacher Quality, there is a consistent positive trend in exam scores as the level increases. Students in the “High” category tend to have higher median scores compared to those in “Medium” or “Low”. However, these differences are small and there is heavy overlap across the score distributions. There are also many outliers present. Looking more specifically at each factor reveals that Access to Resources shows the clearest upward trend in scores, while Family Income shows the least clear trend. Parental Involvement and Teacher Quality both show a subtle upward trend, but substantial overlap remains. Overall, this visualization suggests socioeconomic factors may have some influence on school performance, there is not one single factor alone that is a strong predictor. It is more likely a combination of factors that affects students in the classroom.

How to Read this Visualization

Each panel focuses on one variable, and each box the score distribution for one level of that variable. This makes it easier to compare multiple socioeconomic influences in one place instead of viewing them separately.

Visualization 2: Student Academic Profile by Socioeconomic Status

Plotly Interactive

Takeaway

The spider graph compares the behavioral academic profiles of low, middle, and high socioeconomic status (SES) students across five dimensions that were normalized to a 0-100 scale: attendance, hours studied, tutoring sessions, previous scores, and exam scores. The three SES groups have remarkably similar profiles, with almost complete overlap. The consistency of attendance and hours studied across statuses suggest that student effort and engagement is more uniform than commonly assumed. However, exam score is where meaningful separation occurs. High SES students achieve higher scores than middle SES students, who achieve higher scores than low SES students. Despite similar behavioral inputs (attendance, study hours, and tutoring), those with higher socioeconomic backgrounds still achieve better outcomes in the classroom. This gap shows the influence of more than just individual effort of the student alone.

How to Read this Visualization

Hover over the chart to inspect values for each category and compare the shapes of the three SES groups. The overlapping profiles make it easy to see where the differences are the largest.

Visualization 3: Score Brackets by Family Income

Plotly Interactive

Takeaway

Building on the exam score gap identified in the spider graph, this stacked bar chart shows how the socioeconomic gap plays out in practice through the income group distribution across score brackets. Low income students make up 51.0% of the low scorers and only 36.0% of the high scorers, a stark decrease. High income students have the opposite pattern, with them making up 11.7% of the low scorers and 26.7% of the high scorers. Medium income students remain relatively consistent across the three brackets, peaking at 40.5% of the mid scorers. Income is linked to which end of the score distribution a student ends up in. Low income students are overrepresented at the bottom and underrepresented at the top, while high income students cluster toward the top. This suggests that family income is a structural advantage or disadvantage, influencing a student's chance at reaching high academic achievement.

Interaction/Use

Hover over each segment to see the exact percentage for that income group within a score bracket. This interaction makes the group composition of each performance range much easier to interpret.

Visualization 4: Co-occurrence of Socioeconomic Factors Among Students

D3 Interactive

Takeaway

Unlike the previous visualizations, the arc diagram reveals how socioeconomic factors cluster together within individual students. In the high scorers tab, the arcs are thicker and more densely concentrated among the advantage factors on the left. This means that things like high income, having internet access, high resources, and high parental involvement frequently co-occur in the same students. In the low scorers tab, the thick arcs shift towards the disadvantage factors of low income and low resources, while the advantage factors cluster becomes thinner and sparser. Thus, low scorers not only lack multiple p rotective factors, they also face added socioeconomic challenges. Each missing advantage factor or added disadvantage factor compounds, reinforcing one another and resulting in worse academic performance. On the other hand, high achieving students bring a combination of advantages that accumulate and positively influence outcomes.

Interaction/Use

Use the buttons at the top of the chart to switch between high scorers and low scoreres. Hover over any arc to see the eact pair of factors and the numebrs of students represented. This makes it easier to compare patterns across score groups instead of only looking at one static network.

Visualization 5: Co-occurrence of Socioeconomic Factors Among Students

D3 Interactive

Takeaway

The correlation heatmap serves as a quantitative conclusion to the story built across previous visualizations, showing Pearson r correlations between all academic and behavioral variables across different SES groups. Across all students, attendance (r = 0.58) and hours studied (r = 0.45) are the strongest predictors of exam score, while socioeconomic variables like SES index (r = 0.21), parental involvement (r = 0.16), and access to resources (r = 0.17) show weaker but consistent positive correlations. The SES index is also strongly correlated with family income (r = 0.58) and access to resources (r = 0.54), confirming that these factors cluster together as shown in the Sankey diagram. When filtering by SES group, attendance and hours studied remain the strongest predictors across all groups, reinforcing the spider graph finding that behavioral inputs are consistent regardless of background. However, socioeconomic variables weaken considerably as predictors within each subgroup, suggesting that socioeconomic disadvantage operates most powerfully at the group level, shaping the overall landscape of achievement rather than determining individual outcomes within a group.

Interaction/Use

Use the buttons to switch between high scorer and low scorers, then hover over each flow to see the exact relationship and count.

Summary of Findings

These five visualizations together tell a clear story about how socioeconomic status shapes academic performance. The facet box plots showed that individual socioeconomic factors each have a modest positive association with exam scores, but when filtering to specific score ranges, those differences nearly disappear, suggesting SES shapes which performance tier a student lands in rather than determining outcomes within it. The spider graph revealed that despite being behaviorally similar in effort and engagement, SES groups still diverge on exam score, suggesting something beyond individual effort is at play. The stacked bar chart put numbers to this, showing that low income students are consistently overrepresented among low scorers and underrepresented among high scorers. The Sankey diagram brought school type into the picture, showing that low scoring students are not only concentrated in public schools but also tend to carry multiple compounding disadvantages at once. The correlation heatmap rounded out the analysis by confirming that while attendance and hours studied are the strongest predictors of exam score, socioeconomic factors cluster together in ways that shape the broader environment students perform in.

Taken together, these findings make clear that closing the academic achievement gap is not a one-fix problem. Targeted investment in classroom resources, internet access, and parental support in lower income communities are all worthwhile starting points. Future work could look at which combinations of these factors have the greatest impact on outcomes, and a longitudinal study tracking students over time would help confirm whether improvements in socioeconomic conditions actually translate to better academic performance. Ultimately, this project demonstrates that socioeconomic disadvantage is not a single barrier but an interconnected web of compounding factors, requiring systemic targeted interventions to close the academic gap.
